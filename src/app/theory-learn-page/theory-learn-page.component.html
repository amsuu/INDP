Meaning of Cases

What are cases?

In most Slavic languages, alongside with grammatical gender (like in languages such as German and Spanish) and grammatical number (the difference between “duck” and “ducks”), there is a declension category called a “Case.” These cases help to communicate the of any given word in a given sentence.

And a lot of the times when there is a before a word, the word takes a special case. As you'll see in the Prepositions section. (Don't go there yet! You still need to understand cases.)

To recap, since it is important for you to understand this idea before you read further, just like how in English you can't say “I see he” (Who sees whom??), you have to say “I see him”, in Interslavic you must mark the following seven cases (don't be scared, they're not that complex).

Nominative

This is the default case, the root form almost. It is used in the dictionary form (“lemma form”) of words and when the word in question is the . This is probably the case most intuitive to you and the one you mostly think in as an English speaker.

I speak. — Ja govorjų.

You go. — Ty ideš.

The woman sees. — Žena vidi.

And so on.

Think “Nominative” — “Name”

Accusative

This is the case of the . It is used when the word in question is the direct recipient of the action.

Think “Accusative” — “Accused”

The difference in Interslavic between Nominative and Accusative is somewhat analogous to the difference in English between “he” and “him”. Often, words with this case can be replaced by a simple “him”/“her”.

Wait, what are the “subject” and “direct object”?

Take the English example “I saw my friend.”

Here,
“I” is the subject (the person who is doing the seeing), and
“my friend” is the direct object (the person being seen)

Therefore,
“I” would take the Nominative, and
“my friend” would take the Accusative.

I saw my friend

Ja viděl mojego prijatelja

And finally notice we can replace with “him” here:

I saw him (my friend)

Genitive

Okay, this one is a bit simpler:

The genitive case is most commonly used to show posession. Similar to the English “'s” suffix. That's it!

My colleague's dog.

Pes mojego kolegy.

One last thing: Another way to think about it (which will be helpful later) is as equivilant or corresponding to the English word “of”. As in “the book of knowledge” or “the Cliffs of Dover”.

(The dog of my colleague)

Remember this. I'll even put an interesting icon here so that you can remember this later: table_lamp

Locative

The locative case is even simpler: it shows a location (either metaphorical or literal). For example:

I am at home

Ja jesm v domu

or

In summer it's very hot

V lětu jesť siĺno gorečo

Think “Locative” — “Location”

Dative

Dative is the case of the . To whom the action is being commited. The most common example is with phrases relating to giving something to someone.

Again take an English example “I gave the book to my friend.”

Here,
“I” is the subject (the person who is doing the giving),
“the book” is the direct object (the thing being given), AND
“my friend” is the indirect object (the person to whom the book is being given)

Therefore,
“I” would take the Nominative,
“the book” would take the Accusative, and
“my friend” would take the Dative.

I gave the book  to my friend

Ja dal knigų  mojemu prijatelju

And notice we can replace with “to her” here:

I gave the book  to her

Instrumental

Instrumental is typically used with the meaning of “with” in English. Be it “alongside with” or “by means of”. Examples are:

Idem vškolų s mojimi prijateljami vśaky deń

I go to school with my friends every day

or

Udarjajų gvozd mlatkom

I hit the nail with (using) a hammer

Vocative

Vocative is when you are calling to someone/something and talking directly to them or wanting to grab their attention. For example:

Sine, prihodi malo!
Son, come for a bit!

Čemu ty to sdělal, Dimitre?
Why did you do this, Dimitr?

Bože moj
(Literally) Oh, my God

Think “Vocative” — “Voice”

If you're still confused

Cases aren't that hard of a concept, but actively thinking about them and identifying them, especially as a speaker of a language with barely any (like english), can be daunting at first. For now, I would advise you read through these maybe a couple more times, and then try an exercise I made up:

Take any arbitraty sentence or sentences you can come up with, the longer the more challenging. I'll pick a fairly short example:

I'm at a concert with Jim.

Now, using what you learned above, identify as many cases as you can. (Note that cases should only apply to nouns, names, pronouns and adjectives.)

And before I give you the answer, let's step through the reasoning:

First word: “I'm”. What is this doing? What would this tell me if I had no other context? Well, it would be communicating that “I” is doing something. Which case means “doing something”? To be the actor of a verb? The one doing something? The subject? Well that's the Nominative!

I'm(Nominative)   at a concert with Jim.

Okay, that was pretty easy. Next word is “at”, well... this isn't a noun or adjective, so lets treat this as a “context” word. Words that communicate the same information that cases do. But since English doesn't have the cases to communicate this information, is uses these “context” words. Same goes for “a” this is an article, not a noun. Then we get to our noun: “concert”.

Well... “concert” by itself doesn't really mean anything does it? So let's bring in those context words from before: “at a”. It becomes “at a concert”. Well... Again, What would this communicate to me, with no more context?. It's a location. Something is physically AT a concert. Which case is that? The Locative!

I'm(Nominative)   at a concert(Locative)   with Jim.

Right, then we have “with”. This is a context word. Noted. The next word is “Jim”, this is a name, a PHYSICAL THING OR PERSON, so it must take a case. Bringing in the context: “with Jim”. Which case meant “with”? “Alongside”? The Instrumental!

I'm(Nominative)   at a concert(Locative)   with Jim(Instrumental)

I'll leave a word of warning not to get too caught up in this exercise, and to use simple sentences with it only. The reason is that this is a highly simplified form and its only purpouse is to get you an understanding of cases, where they apply, and how to think about them. It doesn't take into account quirks of language, prepositions, or irregularities. So I would advise doing this with a couple sentences, until you get used to this idea, and moving on.

Here are some example sentences you can try (answers are here):

  • In spring I like to hike with Jess.
    • Hint: Here, ignore “like to hike”. This doesn't take a case.

  • I'll give it to you after I finish it.
    • Hint: Here, ignore “after”. This doesn't change anything. Don't ignore the second “it” though!

  • Greg! I haven't seen you for so long
    • Hint: Here, don't really worry about “for so long”. That's a seperate issue we'll tackle later. Think hard which case “Greg!” takes. He's not doing any action so it can't be the Nominative!

  • The car's engine broke and he's trying to fix it.

  • I love crabs!
